A working estimate for most teams building AI into regulated healthcare content. The failure mode is putting an LLM where a Postgres query or a deterministic rule would do the job faster, cheaper, and with a compliance story that survives procurement.

The frame

CMEfy puts clinicians first and builds in the flow of their day-to-day. You ship 30-second reflection nudges that attach to grand rounds, podcasts, WhatsApp threads, Zoom calls. You run AI Topic Generation against PDFs and articles. The accredited credit lands in Adaptrack. The partner API is the lever. One hundred thousand learners, a million reflective credits, 50+ organizations. A small team against a large surface.

Here is how we would think about your stack. Most teams building AI into regulated healthcare content treat every problem as an AI problem. That is where the budget goes sideways. Around 60 percent of the work inside a product like CMEfy is traditional code and database work. Around 30 percent is rule-based logic. Only around 10 percent is genuine AI work where a model earns its seat. We would come in, map the CMEfy platform against that 60/30/10 view, and hand back a specific plan for which pieces of each workflow belong on which layer.

Three questions we would ask at the booth

  1. When AI Topic Generation output rolls into a new enterprise deployment, who reviews the generated topics against ACCME Standards for Integrity and Independence, and how long does that review take per batch?
  2. Across CMEfy, Learner+, ThinqBits, and Vinea, how much of the backend is shared versus forked, and where does the sharing break down when one team ships fast?
  3. When a health system asks what outcome your platform moved (retention, knowledge, behavior), what is the strongest sentence you can give them today without overstating it?
